# Clay Tending — an algorithmic philosophy

**Clay Tending** is a movement about care made visible. Its canvas is a garden bed rendered
as soft clay — rounded soil blocks with green shoulders, the material language of a toy
world — and its algorithm is stewardship: every visual state on screen is the emergent sum
of small, kind decisions made by the player. Nothing draws itself; everything is grown.

The system is a cellular garden. Each cell carries hidden state — species, growth stage,
moisture, mulch — and a slow clock drains what the player does not tend. Moisture decays
tick by tick; unmulched soil dries twice as fast; a thirsty plant droops through a
continuous wilt curve rather than a binary switch. Growth is quantized (seed, sprout,
young, grown, overgrown) but rendered continuously, every stage easing into the next so
the garden always feels alive between decisions.

Water falls as particles — each droplet an independent body with gravity and a soft
splash — because in this movement, cause must be seen traveling to its effect. Pruning
subtracts: the overgrown state is deliberately shaggy, three extra leaf masses drawn with
seeded jitter, and the shears return the plant to its ideal silhouette with a burst of
sparkle. Mulch is painted as stochastic speckle, forty seeded dots per bed, and quietly
changes the physics underneath.

The mascot is the system's face. Shrubby watches the aggregate — a joy meter integrating
moisture, bloom count, and neglect — and speaks in short clay sentences when thresholds
cross. The player never reads a number; they read a friend.

Every curve here must look tuned by hand: the droop easing, the droplet arc, the wobble
of a freshly planted seedling, the two-frame anticipation before a flower pops. This is
meticulously crafted play physics — the product of countless small iterations, the kind
of master-level restraint where the algorithm disappears and only the tenderness remains.
